WALYALUP BRIDGE: A TURNING POINT
A Deep Audit of Survival, Systems, and What Comes Next

Introduction

Most people wouldn’t expect to see me sleeping rough.
And then there are the ones who would — the ones still holding onto old assumptions and past versions of me, or who were quietly waiting for me to fall.
Either way, here I am.
And here’s the truth of how it happened.
Homelessness doesn’t start the moment you sleep outside.
It starts long before that — in the quiet collapses no one sees, in the pressures you try to absorb, in the moments you tell yourself you can still hold everything together if you just push a little harder.
People imagine a dramatic fall.
A single mistake.
A sudden catastrophe.
But the truth is slower.
Smaller.
More ordinary.
It’s the bills that stack up.
The work that becomes unstable.
The grief that compounds.
The support systems that thin out.
The timing that turns against you.
It’s the way life can narrow, inch by inch, until one day you realise you’ve run out of room to manoeuvre.

1. The Slow Squeeze

It didn’t happen in one dramatic moment.
It was a slow squeeze — unstable work, rising costs, grief, pressure, and the impossible task of holding onto a mortgage while still covering basic living.
Even with a small inheritance.
Even with a privileged start.
Even while trying.
After COVID, after losing both my parents, after losing a partner, the cost of living didn’t just rise — it closed in.
The tipping point?
A $500 bill at the wrong time.
If a roommate had moved in a week earlier…
If one payment had landed sooner…
I would’ve kept the house.
It wasn’t thousands.
It was timing.

2. After the Vehicle

After I lost the vehicle, something changed.
Not just practically — although that was brutal enough.
The car had been shelter, storage, movement, privacy, and one of the last thin layers between me and full exposure.
When it was gone, the city got bigger.
Heavier.
Colder.
Everything I owned had to either be carried, hidden, trusted to someone else, or lost.
And I still had this thought in me:
Get to Fremantle.
Not because it was easy.
Not because I had a plan.
But because Fremantle had always felt significant to me.
A place with memory.
A place with spirit.
A place where maybe — just maybe — one remaining piece of family might still recognise me as human.
So I got off at Fremantle Station.
Technically, that was the arrival point.
Not a bridge.
Not a car park.
The train station.
From there, I walked.

3. The Door

I walked to my sibling’s house and knocked hesitantly.
I wasn’t charging in.
I wasn’t demanding.
I wasn’t trying to take over anyone’s life.
I was just standing there, holding what was left of myself together, hoping for a helping hand from the one remaining part I thought was still family.
And the door shut.
Maybe not literally.
Maybe literally.
At that point, the difference barely mattered.
Because the message landed the same:
Not here.
Not you.
Not now.
And there is a specific kind of silence that happens after that.
Not peaceful silence.
The kind where your body keeps moving because it has to, but something inside you stops speaking for a while.

4. Queen Street

So I walked.
Aimless.
Lost.
Trying not to fully understand what had just happened.
I walked along Queen Street, past FOMO, past St John’s, through a part of Fremantle that already carried layers of my own history.
That area wasn’t random to me.
I knew those streets.
I had watched the new civic centre rise from nothing from the third-floor office nearby.
I had seen the cranes, the concrete, the glass, the scaffolding.
I had watched a whole civic future being built upward while my own life was quietly being stripped down to almost nothing.
That contradiction stayed with me.
A city rebuilding itself.
A person unravelling inside it.
FOMO and St John’s were aware in the way places are aware.
Present.
Watching.
Ambivalent.
The city kept changing.
I kept walking.

5. The Drift

Sleeping rough isn’t just “not having a home.”
You don’t sleep.
You drift.
You stay half-awake, half-alert, always aware of who’s around and where your things are.
Your belongings aren’t really yours.
They get stolen.
Moved.
Soaked.
Taken.
Cleared away.
You can lose everything in a single night.
And when that happens, people still expect you to function like nothing has changed.
Charge your phone.
Keep appointments.
Answer messages.
Apply for things.
Stay calm.
Stay clean.
Stay reasonable.
But survival mode doesn’t leave much room for paperwork.

6. The Winter Night

One freezing night in the middle of winter changed something in me.
I was trying to look after two other men in the side walkways of Paddy Troy Mall — one elderly and sick, one paralytic and incoherent.
Between the three of us, we had two single sleeping bags.
I did what I could.
Kept them sheltered.
Kept them warm.
Kept them alive.
Then at approximately 3 AM, we received a move-on notice.
For anyone unfamiliar with the term, a move-on notice is a police direction requiring a person to leave a location and not return for a specified period.
Now consider the situation plainly:
Three vulnerable people.
In winter.
Trying to survive the night.
No disturbance.
No threat.
No harm.
And the practical outcome was this:
Move on into the cold with nowhere meaningful to go.
That was the moment something snapped — not into despair, but into clarity.
A realisation that the system often treats survival itself as an inconvenience.

7. A Notorious Name

There are names on the street that carry weight — not because they’re feared, but because they’re known.
Recognised.
Repeated.
Passed along like coordinates in a landscape where trust is rare and reputation becomes currency.
One man I’d met weeks earlier, back when I was still fully dressed up — clean, sharp, put together — used to joke, half serious and half cheeky, that I was the only man he’d ever wanted to marry.
But the night everything changed, he wasn’t the man I’d first met.
He had the flu.
A chest infection.
He’d just been released from hospital with nothing — no belongings, no bedding, no support, no plan.
I found him outside the kebab shop, shivering, exhausted, barely holding himself upright.
That’s why he had nothing with him that night under the walkway at Paddy Troy Mall.
Not because he was careless.
Not because he was irresponsible.
Because he had been discharged back onto the street with no meaningful support structure in place.
After that night — after trying to keep him warm, after the move-on notice, after the chaos — something shifted between us.
He became a kind of street dad to me.
Not sentimental.
Not symbolic.
Just real.
He checked on me.
He steadied me.
He reminded me I wasn’t invisible.
And in a world that kept taking from me — belongings, safety, people, dignity — he gave me something rare:
A sense that I wasn’t alone.
A notorious presence, yes.
But also a steady one.
A presence that meant something in a place where meaning is hard to come by.

8. Walyalup Bridge & The Diamond Soul Constellation

Eventually, Walyalup Bridge became more than infrastructure.
A place between versions of myself.
Not home. Not shelter. Not safety.
But a point on the map where I could no longer pretend this was just a rough patch.
It was a turning point.
The bridge held the truth plainly: I had fallen through the cracks, but I had not disappeared.
I was still observing. Still thinking. Still connecting systems. Still seeing what most people are trained not to see.
And that’s where something else crystallised — something that had been forming quietly for years, long before the collapse, long before the vehicle, long before the door shut.
The Diamond Soul Constellation.
Not as a brand. Not as a project. Not as a pitch. But as a way of seeing.
A framework built from lived experience, pressure, identity, care, and the invisible architecture that shapes how people survive — or don’t — inside a city.
The DSC didn’t arrive as a grand idea. It arrived as a survival instinct. A way to map what was happening to me. A way to understand the patterns. A way to turn pain into evidence, and evidence into design.
Standing under Walyalup Bridge, I could see the whole thing clearly:
How quickly stability collapses.
How easily people become invisible.
How much survival depends on timing, relationships, infrastructure, and whether anyone decides you are still worth helping.
The Diamond Soul Constellation wasn’t theory. It was the only language big enough to hold what was happening.
A system for identity. A system for care. A system for value. A system for community. A system for the people who fall through the cracks — and the ones who pretend not to see them.
Walyalup Bridge wasn’t the end of the story. It was the point where the personal became structural. Where the collapse became a map. Where the map became a constellation. And where the constellation finally had a place to speak.

9. What This Exposed

This isn’t just a personal story. It’s a systems audit.
Because once you experience homelessness directly, you start seeing all the invisible assumptions built into society.
That people have somewhere to charge a phone. Somewhere to shower. Somewhere to store belongings. Somewhere to rest without being moved on. Somewhere safe to receive information. Somewhere stable enough to complete forms, attend appointments, and recover from shock.
But if you don’t have those things, the system often treats your instability as non-compliance.
You miss the call. You lose the form. Your phone dies. Your belongings vanish. Your sleep breaks. Your nervous system overloads.
Because when instability compounds, one event can trigger an entire cascade.
A lost bag is not just a lost bag. It can mean: No ID. No charger. No medication. No dry clothes. No way to contact support. No proof of appointments. No continuity.
From the outside, people see disorganisation.
From the inside, it is often chain-reaction survival.
One removal. One missed call. One dead phone. One unsafe night. One broken connection.
And the system still asks why people did not manage better.
That is not support. That is a maze with the floor removed.

10. When She Returned — The Chaos Cascade

She reappeared in my life suddenly — not planned, not expected, not in a way that made sense.
It had been months since I last saw her, the chaos carrying its own gravity even back. The image of her straggler running toward the train as the siren and light bore down burns in my memory — the end to the first. Maybe I'd ask my inner dialogue since I still had a traditional plan or believed that's what I wanted, then she was gone, orbiting other people, other houses, other gravitational pulls. Victim or condemned by the more extreme of the judicial move-ons: a 6-month or longer blanket ban on ALL Western Australian entertainment precincts.
The next she was back beside me, already tangled in a situation too unstable for either of us to properly contain. And I fell straight into a familiar cycle.
We spent several nights in an underground carpark — one of the only places that felt remotely predictable after dark.
Concrete. Cameras. Routine. A bunker beneath the city.
For a moment, it felt like a pause. A fragile pocket of safety carved out of urban survival. I could time my entry — I saw a locked fortress sealing the world outside and me… Us in alone unless I let them. A lock and door, a roof and no weather, heaven…
Then the third night changed everything.
Something was wrong almost immediately.
My body stopped responding normally. My memory fractured. My awareness came in flashes instead of continuity.
Whether I had been intentionally exposed to something unknowingly, or experienced a severe physiological collapse, I cannot state with certainty.
What I can say with certainty is this:
I became severely disoriented. My consciousness flickered in and out. My coordination collapsed. And the night deteriorated rapidly around us.
I remember fragments. Her. Other men. Movement. Arguments. Confusion. Shadows crossing concrete under fluorescent light. And it was only in rewriting an edit of the section I realise the whole possible nightmare.
The next day I left the carpark briefly to get food, water, and supplies in an attempt to stabilise the situation.
When I returned, security had removed everything. Her belongings. My belongings. Everything. No warning. No retrieval process. No opportunity to recover what mattered.
What followed was not stability but escalation. The atmosphere around us became volatile. Arguments intensified. Accusations spiralled. At one point weapons were produced during the conflict.
Everyone involved was exhausted, unstable, emotionally overloaded, or operating under conditions that no healthy environment should normalise. The entire situation felt less like rational decision-making and more like a systems failure unfolding in real time.
And underneath all of it sat the same structural reality:
When people lose safety, sleep, belongings, continuity, and support simultaneously, chaos does not arrive as an exception. It becomes the environment itself.
Eventually she stayed behind in that orbit while I kept moving.
Later she returned again — shaken, exhausted, still trying to hold onto herself through the instability.
We spent a day together in a strange pocket of temporary calm. A tent filled with books, diagrams, symbolic clutter, half-finished ideas, and survival fragments. It was the day the photos were taken. Not curated. Not posed. Just two people caught in a moment of accidental honesty.
But the pattern repeated. No stable shelter. No secure fallback. No meaningful safety.
At one point we ended up sheltering in hospital toilets simply because they were warm, tiled, lit, and temporarily protected from the outside world.
And eventually I reached the moment that broke something inside me.
There was transport waiting for me. A safe place for the night. But only for me. Not for us. Not for her. Just me.
If I stayed where we were, I knew I would collapse completely. So I walked away.
Not because I wanted to. Not because it felt morally clean. Because survival sometimes demands choices that feel indistinguishable from betrayal.
As the vehicle pulled away, the city blurred into light and concrete, and the truth settled heavily inside me: This was the pivot. This was the moment that led directly to Flamingo Manor.

11. Flamingo Manor — The Threshold

The ride dropped me at a place I only half-expected to find: a pink house on a hill. A name I’d heard in passing. A promise of a couch and a place to land.
I wasn’t expecting anything except a roof and a moment to breathe.
When the door opened, the world tilted. I wasn’t stepping into a quiet lounge room or a spare mattress. I was stepping into a room full of people — twenty, maybe more — all culturally alive, expressive, creative, socially fluent in ways I hadn’t been around in years.
People who spoke in colour and metaphor and rhythm. People who built worlds with their hands and voices. People who had not been living inside the slow suffocation of prolonged isolation.
The noise. The brightness. The coincidence. The timing. The surreal fact that I had walked out of the darkest stretch of my life and straight into a room full of people who actually knew how to listen.
I was overloaded. Cracked open. Still carrying the wrong parts of my story. Still half-feral from the street.
And somehow — impossibly — I ended up telling them the truth. Not the polished version. Not the safe version. The real version.
And the room didn’t flinch. The room didn’t recoil. The room didn’t treat me like a problem to be solved. They listened. Not with pity. Not with fear. With presence.
And in that moment something tectonic shifted. Because for the first time in years I wasn’t choosing the devil-you-know. I wasn’t choosing collapse. I was choosing the unknown. I was choosing the threshold.
Flamingo Manor wasn’t salvation. It was a pivot. A fault-line. A moment where the story stopped collapsing inward and started turning outward again.

12. What Comes Next

I don’t want this story to become pity. I want it to become structure.
Because Fremantle can do better. Not through grand speeches. Through practical, visible, humane design.
Practical changes that would help:
• Public charging.
• Public showers.
• Clear notice before belongings are removed.
• Transparent retrieval systems.
• Safe storage options.
• Better communication.
• Community spaces that don’t quietly erase the people most in need of them.
And beyond that — deeper civic imagination.
Walyalup doesn’t need to become a polished postcard version of itself. It needs to remember what made it alive in the first place: street culture, artists, markets, performers, oddballs, builders, survivors. People with nowhere else to go and something real to offer.
A city is not measured by how well it photographs. It is measured by what happens to people when they fall.

13. Closing

So yes, this is personal. It is about losing housing. Losing the vehicle. Getting off the train at Fremantle Station. Walking to family. Having the door shut. Wandering Queen Street. Watching a civic centre rise in memory while my own life collapsed in real time.
But it is also bigger than me.
Walyalup Bridge became the place where survival turned into signal. Where pain became evidence. Where evidence became design. Where design could still become care.
I am not telling this because I have neatly risen above it. I am telling it from inside the fracture.
Because sometimes the person sleeping rough under the bridge is not the end of the story.
Sometimes they are the witness. Sometimes they are the warning. And sometimes — if the city is brave enough to listen — they are carrying the blueprint for what comes next.
