WALYALUP BRIDGE: A TURNING POINT

A true account of survival, community, dignity and what comes next.

This is not a story asking for pity. It is a story about what happens when people fall through the cracks — and what becomes possible when community steps in before a person disappears entirely.

The Slow Squeeze

It didn’t happen in one dramatic moment. It was a slow squeeze — unstable work, rising costs, and the impossible task of holding onto a mortgage while still covering basic living. The tipping point? A $500 bill at the wrong time. If a roommate had moved in a week earlier… If one payment had landed sooner… I would’ve kept the house. It wasn’t thousands. It was timing.

The Drift

Sleeping rough isn’t just “not having a home.” You don’t sleep — you drift. You stay half-awake, half-alert, always aware of who’s around and where your things are. Your belongings aren’t really yours. They get stolen. Moved. Soaked. Or taken in council cleanups. You can lose everything in a single night. And when it rains… whatever the theft didn’t take… the rain dissolves.

The Winter Night

One freezing night in the middle of winter changed something in me. I was trying to look after two other men in the side walkways of Paddy Troy Mall — one elderly and sick, one paralytic and incoherent. Between the three of us, we had two single sleeping bags. I did what I could. Kept them sheltered. Kept them warm. Kept them alive. Then at 3 AM, we got a move-on notice. No trouble. No complaint. Just trying to survive the cold. That was the moment something snapped — not in despair, but in rage at the helplessness. At the way the system treats survival like an inconvenience.

"This story did not end under a bridge. Through the support of people in Fremantle, including the community around Community of Kindness, survival became recovery — and recovery became a reason to help build something better."
The Crystallisation

But standing there in the cold… something else formed. I realised the pressure wasn’t breaking me — it was compressing me. Like diamond formation: intense pressure creating clarity. That night became the start of a new kind of logic. A new way of seeing the patterns.

The Diamond Soul Constellation

Even out here, I haven’t lost who I am. I still see systems. Patterns. Ways things could be better. Over the past few years, I’ve been developing a framework I call the Diamond Soul Constellation — ideas around identity, community, and technology, built from lived experience and systemic insight. But when your day revolves around whether your gear has been stolen, seized, or soaked… it’s almost impossible to operate at the level you know you can.

The Latest Hit

And the pressure doesn’t stop. Today, while I was out trying to feed myself, Wilson’s Parking took everything of value and importance I had left. Not just items — anchors. The things that keep you stable enough to function. Gone. I’m walking with one shoe, a guitar, and a backpack full of empty power banks. The antibiotics I was meant to be taking? The scripts I needed to fill? Lost days ago in the chaos of trying to keep myself and someone else safe. Last night I took the girl I care about to a safe house — I thought it was a moment of stability. Now she’s with a friend and the son of the house, and I’m back out here again, trying to hold myself together with nothing charged, nothing warm, nothing certain. Collapse doesn’t happen once. It happens in waves. It happens in spirals. It happens in the small moments — the missing shoe, the empty battery, the lost medication — that add up until you’re standing on the edge of something dark and you have to decide whether you’re going to fall or build something from the pressure.

The Turning Point

I don’t want to go “back to normal.” Normal already failed. What I need is a reset point — enough stability to breathe, think clearly, and start building again. A laptop so I can use the skills I’ve spent years developing. A safe enough base to turn insight into action. Because this isn’t the end. It’s a turning point. I’m still here. Still watching the patterns across Fremantle. Still believing we can build better bridges than the ones we’re sleeping under.
